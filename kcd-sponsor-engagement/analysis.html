1.The situation & the so-what

synvert xgeeks doesn't just sponsor KCD Porto — we are the team behind it (we curate, organise and promote the event). That organiser role is, in business terms, an asset we have not yet monetised: warm relationships with 60+ cloud-native companies, ~€59K of closed 2025 sponsorships (~€33K in 2024), ~20 returning sponsors — plus bulk-ticket-buyer contacts (Siemens, NOS and others) and a full 2024–2025 speaker database, many of whom work at top cloud and AI companies. Every signed sponsorship also gave us the name of a budget-holding decision-maker.

These are streaming, fintech, automotive, retail-tech and product-engineering organisations running Kubernetes and cloud-native stacks — precisely the buyer xgeeks sells nearshore engineering to.

The recommendation. Run a deliberate dual-track motion. Fábio keeps securing 2026 sponsorships (Track A). In parallel, a separate xgeeks business voice opens commercial conversations (Track B) across many channels — email/LinkedIn before, booth/dinners/lunches/cocktails during, national meetups and our ongoing logistics relationship year-round — governed by a per-account state matrix, double-opt-in hand-offs, and a shared BD CRM. The dinners matter, but they are one door among many. Measure on € pipeline influenced over 6–12 months at ≥4:1 — never contact counts.

2.Plain-language glossary

  • ABM (account-based marketing) — instead of chasing many leads, pick a short list of specific high-value companies and treat each as its own mini-campaign.
  • Tier — how much hands-on effort an account earns: Tier 1 = deepest 1:1, Tier 2 = lighter segment treatment, Tier 3 = light-touch nurture.
  • Target — the person who signs the contract / holds the budget. We often have no direct contact with them.
  • Route-in — the warm path to the target: the sponsor's booking contact and any other relationships we've built inside that organisation.
  • Double opt-in — never connect two people cold; the introducer first checks both sides are happy to be connected, then makes the intro.
  • "Pipeline influenced, not lead volume" — measure the € value of real conversations and potential deals opened, not vanity counts like contacts collected or badges scanned.
  • "Slot into the gaps" — time business conversations for moments that don't clash with closing the sponsorship or running the event.

4.The strategic insight

The single most important idea: we hold trusted access to a room full of our exact buyers, and doors open by working that access across many touchpoints — before, during and after KCD — not at one big moment.

  • Before is the biggest window: email, LinkedIn, our existing logistics line, the speaker DB and ticket buyers — warmed weeks ahead. This is where most meetings are actually booked.
  • During offers many touchpoints: our booth, walking sponsors' booths, venue lunches and cocktails, and the two dinners. The dinners are one valuable asset — not the whole plan.
  • After / year-round is where deals mature: national meetups keep relationships warm, with opportunities closing over 6–18 months.

A well-run executive dinner of 10–20 named accounts can influence €0.5–2M of pipeline — but only because it sits inside a continuous motion that warms accounts beforehand. Every touch stays on neutral ground: relationship-building, never selling from the stage.

5.The dual-track rule, the state matrix & the hand-off

We wear two hats with the same people: event organiser and engineering-services vendor. The rule: sponsorship first; never pay-to-play (either direction); separate the voices — Fábio is the organiser / sponsorship-closer and stays in that role; a different voice opens business.

5.2 The hand-off — double opt-in, never cold

All three patterns use established warm-intro best practice: double opt-in (ask both sides first), a forwardable blurb (1–2 lines on each person, then "I'll let you two take it from here"), and timing separated from any sponsorship/renewal ask so it never feels quid-pro-quo.

Pattern A — Fábio warm intro, then steps back

Fábio first checks both sides are open, then connects softly and exits. It must read peer-to-peer, never transactional. It works even when the sponsorship is not closed, was a "no", or went unanswered — as long as prior contact exists and they know Fábio. Example:

"Hi [name] — enjoyed working with you on KCD again. Separate from all that: a colleague of mine, [Gui/Ivan], leads our platform/cloud work at xgeeks and I thought you two would just enjoy comparing notes on how you're scaling [X]. Mind if I connect you? No agenda at all." — then a two-line blurb on each, and "I'll let you two take it from here."

Pattern B — direct peer outreach (recommended default)

Note: Ivan is Sales, not engineering. Ivan (or the right manager) messages the route-in directly, leading with the KCD/community tie — never "Fábio gave me your email, let's talk business." De-awkwarding it: the relationship is KCD, the topic is engineering, the ask is a peer chat. Example:

"Hi [name] — I'm with the team behind KCD Porto, and on the xgeeks engineering side. Loved having [company] involved. We do a lot of nearshore platform/cloud work and I saw you're scaling [X] — would a 20-minute swap of notes be useful? Genuinely no-pressure, peer to peer."

Is handing over emails awkward? Only if it's done cold. Double opt-in removes the awkwardness: the recipient has effectively pre-agreed, and the first line earns the right to write (we run their event; we're peers in the same field).

Pattern C — on-the-floor (the most natural, most-used)

The richest channel. Fábio can give intros and physically walk people to booths. Ivan (Sales) pre-curates a check-in list beforehand — which sponsors and which attendees to seek out — so the team knows exactly who to talk to. On-site surfaces: our booth, sponsors' booths, and venue lunches and cocktails (note: the dinners themselves are off-site). A manager talks shop as a peer; business emerges naturally. Fábio is the host, not the salesperson.

9.Channel playbook — the doors

Multi-channel and year-round; KCD is the warm start, not the whole plan.

9.1 Before the event — remote & ahead-of-time (the biggest window)

Email & LinkedIn outreach (most important)

Weeks ahead, the business voice sends a peer message opened with the "we're the team behind KCD Porto" credibility and a soft transition to "could we help with X". One reusable template works across sponsors, speakers and ticket buyers (see §10). Pre-warming makes on-site meetings far more likely.

The existing logistics relationship

Through KCD we already talk to sponsors constantly — onboarding, info, terms, payments, invoices, booth, logistics. A genuinely warm, ongoing line. Once a sponsorship is closed, the business track can open off this relationship — through the separate business voice, never mixed into a logistics email, per the state matrix.

Bulk-ticket buyers

Companies such as Siemens and NOS bought blocks of tickets; those contacts are already gathered and have been spoken to. Because Fábio shouldn't run sales, he hands each to Ivan (Sales), who follows up with a KCD hook → "since your team's coming, worth a quick chat about how you scale engineering?" → exploratory call.

Past speakers (2024–2025 database)

Many past speakers work at top cloud and AI companies — KCD's exact topic. Reach out ahead using the speaker database, framed around their talk and the community, leading to business only where there's real fit. The same database feeds 2026 CFP and meetup speakers.

9.2 During the event — on-site, peer-to-peer

The booth — a first-class channel

We have a booth, and sponsors are on-site all day — heads and managers are often physically present. Staff our booth with sales + engineering managers who walk over to sponsor booths, ask about their stack and what they're building, and host the same curious conversations at ours. Get to know their engineers; understand the challenges. Curiosity first, no pitch.

Venue lunches & cocktails

Beyond the booth, the lunches and cocktails at the venue are natural, low-pressure surfaces to meet sponsors and attendees. Ivan pre-curates a check-in list of who to seek out (sponsors and flagged attendees) so on-site time is spent well.

The speaker & guest dinners — one asset, not the plan

  • Speaker dinner — held on Day 1 or Day 2 of KCD; peers, speakers and partners. (The dinners are off-site.)
  • Guest dinner — composition: we invite all sponsors, plus a few curated speakers (not necessarily all), plus a few of xgeeks's own happy clients to make the room look strong (faz bonito). Aim for the senior signatories or someone who can reach them.
  • How we run it: our managers simply present and work the room naturally — no assigned seating, no scripts. Deploy by strength (below).

10.Introducing the business topic — concrete wording

The framing is always peers in engineering discovering a need. The remote, ahead-of-time messages matter most, and all carry the "team behind KCD Porto" credibility.

Before — email / LinkedIn (reusable across sponsors, speakers & ticket buyers)

"Hi [name] — I'm with the team behind KCD Porto, great to have [company] involved again. Wearing my other hat: I'm on the engineering side at xgeeks and we do a lot of nearshore platform/cloud work. I saw you're scaling [specific thing] — would a 20-minute swap of notes before November be useful? No agenda, just peers."

Before — bulk-ticket buyer (via Ivan, Sales)

"Hi [name] — from the KCD Porto team. Since your folks are joining, I thought I'd reach out from the xgeeks engineering side — we help teams like yours scale delivery. Worth a quick exploratory call before the event?"

During — at our booth or theirs

"What are you folks building these days? … We run a lot of that nearshore — how's the platform side holding up as you scale?" Curiosity first; let the need surface.

During — at the dinner (match the person)

Openers (Soares / André / Helder / Ivan) build rapport. When it turns technical: "let me grab Gui — he's deep in exactly this." Gui / Gonçalo go deep and, where it fits, suggest a follow-up. No scripts — natural peer conversation.

Never. Imply that sponsoring buys business (or vice-versa); sell from the stage; pitch a competitor. Off-stage, peer-to-peer, vendor-neutral — always. Respect the CNCF Code of Conduct and attendee privacy.

13.Measurement

Measure like any acquisition channel, and report in € of pipeline and revenue influenced — not contact counts, MQLs or badge scans.

  • Tier-1 (warm + reach) and Tier-2 accounts engaged — target vs. actual.
  • Qualified conversations / real needs surfaced (benchmark 5–8 per dinner).
  • Meetings booked in the 30 days post-event (benchmark 8–14).
  • Opportunities created; € pipeline influenced over a 6–12 month window.
  • Pipeline-to-cost ratio ≥ 4:1.
  • Cost per qualified conversation.

All tracked in the BD CRM, with every contact tagged to KCD 2026 so we can later report "this event opened X opportunities and Y closed-won."
